# Behavioural Evaluation Framework for AI Judgement Systems

This repository accompanies the working paper:

**Hull, I. (2026). _The Behavioural Evaluation Framework for AI Judgement Systems_. Beyond the Average Research Series.**

The project explores how AI systems behave when the same evaluative task is repeated under controlled conditions. Rather than focusing only on individual outputs, the framework examines patterns that emerge across repeated executions, internal signals and comparisons between independent systems.

---

# Overview

Large language models are increasingly used to interpret text, classify information and support decisions that may affect individuals or organisations. Many evaluation approaches examine single outputs, assessing whether responses appear accurate, unbiased or well explained.

However, language-based AI systems generate responses probabilistically. When the same task is repeated, the resulting outputs and reasoning may vary.

The **Behavioural Evaluation Framework** proposes examining AI judgement systems through three complementary perspectives:

1. **Repeated execution**  
   Running the same evaluative task multiple times to observe stability, drift or fragmentation in judgement.

2. **Internal signals**  
   Examining indicators such as confidence, agreement or other internal evaluation signals to determine whether they track behavioural changes.

3. **Independent system comparison**  
   Comparing how different AI systems interpret the same input to reveal differences in judgement thresholds or interpretation.

Together, these perspectives allow evaluation to focus on **behavioural stability** rather than isolated answers.

---

# Research Context

The framework emerged from experiments examining how large language models evaluate job advertisements for potential age-coded language.

Repeated evaluations revealed behavioural patterns that are not visible when examining single outputs, including:

- stable judgement across repeated runs  
- convergence towards similar interpretations  
- drift in reasoning across executions  
- fragmentation where different runs produce substantially different conclusions

These observations suggest that reliability in AI judgement systems may be better understood through **behaviour observed over time**.
